Why Is My Whirlpool Dryer Not Heating?

When a Whirlpool dryer tumbles normally but the clothes come out cold and damp, the problem is nearly always in the heating circuit rather than the motor or control board. Whirlpool's electric dryers rely on a looped circuit that includes the heating element, a thermal fuse, a cycling thermostat, and a high-limit thermostat. If any single link breaks, the heat stops — but the drum keeps spinning because the drive motor runs on a separate circuit.

For gas Whirlpool dryers, the heat source is a burner assembly. The most common failure points there are the igniter and the gas valve coils (also called radiant sensors and solenoid coils). Our lead technician Alex M. notes that gas valve coil failure is extremely common in older Whirlpool gas dryers and is often misdiagnosed as an igniter problem.

What Are the Top Causes of a Whirlpool Dryer Not Heating?

  • Blown thermal fuse — The single most common cause. The thermal fuse is a one-time safety device that opens permanently when the dryer overheats. Once blown, it must be replaced.
  • Failed heating element — The resistance wire inside the element breaks over time, interrupting the circuit. Visually it may look intact, but an ohmmeter will show no continuity.
  • Defective cycling thermostat — This thermostat regulates temperature during operation. When it fails open, the heating element never receives power.
  • High-limit thermostat failure — A backup safety device that cuts heat when the exhaust temperature is dangerously high. It can fail in the open position, killing heat permanently.
  • Blocked dryer vent — A clogged vent raises internal temperatures and trips the thermal fuse. If you don't clear the vent before replacing the fuse, the new fuse will blow again quickly.
  • Gas valve coil failure (gas models) — The coils energise the valve to release gas for ignition. When they fail, the igniter glows but gas never flows.

Confirm the symptom

Test the thermal fuse first

Using a multimeter we test the thermal fuse for continuity. No continuity means it has blown and needs replacement. We also record the exhaust temperature to see if the vent is restricted.

3

Test heating element and thermostats

If the fuse is intact, we test the element and both thermostats for continuity. A failed element will read OL (open loop) on the meter.

4

Inspect and clear the vent system

Regardless of what failed, we inspect the full vent run for lint blockage. A restricted vent is the root cause of most thermal fuse failures.

5

Replace the failed component and test

We replace the faulty part with an OEM or OEM-equivalent component, reassemble the dryer, and run a full heat cycle before leaving.

Can I Fix a No-Heat Whirlpool Dryer Myself?

Replacing a thermal fuse is technically straightforward if you are comfortable disassembling appliances — the part costs under $20 at most appliance parts suppliers. However, there are two important caveats. First, if the vent is clogged (which it almost always is when a thermal fuse blows), you need to clear it or the new fuse will fail again within weeks. Second, for gas dryers, working on any gas components requires a TSSA licence in Ontario. Attempting gas work without a licence is illegal and dangerous.

Warning: Never operate a Whirlpool gas dryer with the gas supply connected while performing diagnostics unless you are a TSSA-licensed technician. Gas leaks during appliance repair are a fire and explosion hazard. If you smell gas at any point, leave the area immediately and call your gas utility.

The most common cause is a blown thermal fuse, which cuts power to the heating element as a safety measure when the dryer overheats. Other frequent causes include a failed heating element, a faulty high-limit thermostat, or — on gas models — a defective gas valve coil.
