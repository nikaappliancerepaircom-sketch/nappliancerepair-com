What AI Dispatch Actually Does for a Repair Business

The term "AI dispatch" sounds technical, but the core function is straightforward: it takes every incoming service request — from your website booking form, a phone call transcription, or a text message — and automatically assigns it to the right technician based on live data.

A modern AI dispatch system evaluates several variables simultaneously when a new job comes in:

  • Technician location — which tech is physically closest to the customer right now
  • Current schedule — how many jobs are already booked for today and tomorrow
  • Skill match — does this tech specialize in the appliance brand or type being requested
  • Parts inventory — does the van stock the likely parts for this type of repair
  • Customer history — has this customer been served before and do they have specific preferences

The system makes this assignment in seconds and sends a booking confirmation to the customer automatically. No back-and-forth. No human error. No calls that go unanswered because the owner is under a dishwasher in Etobicoke.

What to Look for in an AI Dispatch Tool for Appliance Repair

Not all field service software is built for the appliance repair market specifically. General-purpose scheduling tools lack the job-type logic that a repair business needs. When evaluating platforms, look for these capabilities:

  • Appliance-specific job types — the system should understand that a "refrigerator not cooling" is a different job profile than a "washer not draining," with different likely duration and parts requirements
  • Mobile-first technician app — techs need to accept jobs, log arrival, take photos, and collect signatures from their phone, not a desktop in the office
  • Integrated invoicing — dispatch and billing should live in the same system so there is no double-entry between platforms
  • Customer communication automation — confirmations, reminders, and follow-up reviews should send without manual intervention
  • Toronto-area mapping — the routing engine needs to understand GTA traffic patterns, not just straight-line distances
