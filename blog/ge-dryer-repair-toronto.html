What Are the Most Common GE Dryer Problems in Toronto?

GE dryers have a reliable mechanical design that holds up well over time, but several components have predictable wear patterns. Our technicians see these issues regularly across GE units in Toronto:

  • Not heating — blown thermal fuse — The most common service call on GE electric dryers. The thermal fuse is a one-time safety device located on the heating element housing or exhaust duct that opens permanently when the dryer overheats.
  • Not heating — failed heating element — The element coil breaks, interrupting the heating circuit. A visual inspection may not reveal the break; a multimeter test is required.
  • Taking too long to dry — Usually caused by a clogged exhaust vent, a damaged drum seal allowing air bypass, or a faulty moisture sensor that ends cycles too early.
  • Not spinning — broken drum belt — The drum belt snaps after years of use. The motor runs and the dryer heats, but the drum doesn't turn. Clothes come out hot and damp in a pile.
  • Squealing or grinding noise — Worn drum bearing or drum support rollers create a squealing sound that progressively worsens. If ignored, the bearing can seize and damage the drum itself.
  • Not starting — A faulty door switch (the latch switch that confirms the door is closed) prevents the cycle from beginning. Also check the start relay and control board.

Why Is My GE Dryer Taking Too Long to Dry?

Slow drying is one of the most common complaints we receive for GE dryers in Toronto, and the cause is almost always the exhaust vent system rather than the dryer itself. Toronto's older housing stock — particularly the semi-detached homes of North York and Scarborough — often has long, flexible foil vent runs that collect lint rapidly. When the vent becomes restricted, the dryer cannot expel moist air efficiently, and drying times double or triple.

Before calling for service on a slow-drying GE dryer, clean the lint screen thoroughly and check that the exterior vent cap opens freely when the dryer is running. If vent cleaning doesn't resolve the problem, the drum seal, moisture sensor, or heating element may require inspection.

How Do We Diagnose a GE Dryer Not Heating?

1

Test the thermal fuse

The thermal fuse is the first component tested on any no-heat call. We locate it (usually on the exhaust duct or heating element housing) and test for continuity with a multimeter. A blown fuse reads OL.

2

Check the vent for blockage

We measure exhaust temperature and airflow at the exterior vent cap. Restricted airflow is recorded and the customer is advised before we proceed — clearing the vent is part of a complete thermal fuse repair.

3

Test the heating element

If the thermal fuse is intact, we test the element for continuity. A broken element reads open circuit on the meter, confirming replacement is needed.

4

Test cycling thermostat and high-limit thermostat

Both thermostats are tested for correct continuity. A failed cycling thermostat will prevent the element from receiving power even if the fuse and element are both functional.

Warning: A GE electric dryer operates on a 240V circuit. Before accessing the heating element housing or any rear panel, verify that the dryer is unplugged — not just switched off. A 240V shock can be lethal. Never work inside a dryer cabinet while it is connected to power.
