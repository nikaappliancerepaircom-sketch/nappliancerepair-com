A washing machine that refuses to drain is one of those problems that escalates fast: you open the lid to find clothes sitting in a full tub of dirty water, and suddenly your whole laundry schedule is derailed. We see this call frequently across Toronto, Scarborough, Mississauga, and North York — and in the majority of cases, the fix is simpler than homeowners expect.

This guide walks through the four most common causes of a washer not draining, with a step-by-step diagnostic checklist you can work through before deciding whether to call a technician.

Step-by-Step Diagnostic Checklist

Before opening anything up, check the obvious. A washer that won't drain is sometimes the result of a setting or a simple oversight:

1

Check the Error Code

Modern washers display drain-related error codes: Samsung shows "5E" or "SE," LG shows "OE," Whirlpool shows "F21." Look up your specific model's code in the manual or search the error code + your brand online. This narrows the diagnosis immediately.

2

Check the Drain Hose for Kinks

Pull the washer away from the wall and visually inspect the drain hose — the corrugated hose running from the back of the machine to the standpipe or utility sink. A single sharp kink can completely block water flow. Straighten any kinks and ensure the hose end sits no more than 8 inches down into the standpipe.

3

Clean the Drain Pump Filter

Front-load washers have a small access panel at the lower front. Open it, place a shallow tray underneath, and slowly unscrew the filter cap. Debris — coins, hair, lint, small socks — commonly collect here and block the pump. Clean the filter and thread, then reinstall firmly. This solves the problem in about 40% of no-drain calls.

4

Test the Lid Switch (Top-Loaders)

Top-load washers won't drain if the lid switch fails, because the machine interprets an open lid as a safety condition. Press and hold the small plastic switch near the lid opening manually — if the drum starts draining, the switch is your problem. Replacement switches cost $15–$40 and are straightforward to swap.

Cause 1: Clogged Drain Pump Filter

As noted above, the pump filter is the first place to check on any front-loader. Most manufacturers recommend cleaning this filter every 3–4 months, but most people never do — so by the time it blocks entirely, it may be packed solid with debris.

Pro Tip: Keep an old towel and a shallow baking dish near your washer so you're ready to catch the water when you open the filter access. A fully blocked filter can release up to 2 litres of water.

Cause 2: Kinked or Blocked Drain Hose

Beyond physical kinks, drain hoses can become partially clogged with soap scum, lint, and mineral deposits over years of use. If straightening the hose doesn't solve the problem, disconnect it and run water through it with a garden hose outdoors. If water flow is restricted, replace the hose (typically $15–$25 at any hardware store).

The hose height also matters: if the drain hose outlet is lower than the drum, water will siphon back into the machine. The drain hose exit point should be between 24 and 48 inches from the floor.

Cause 3: Faulty Lid Switch

Lid switches are a common failure point on top-load washers that are 5+ years old. The switch is activated each time the lid closes — thousands of times over a machine's life. When it fails, the machine's safety logic prevents spin and drain cycles from running.

You can test continuity with a multimeter set to the ohms setting. A functioning switch shows near-zero resistance when pressed and infinite resistance when released. No reading in either position means the switch needs replacing. This is a repair our technicians complete in under 30 minutes on most models.

Cause 4: Drain Pump Failure

If the filter is clear, the hose is unobstructed, and the lid switch tests fine, the drain pump itself may have failed. Listen for the pump during a drain cycle — you should hear a low hum and water movement. Complete silence suggests electrical failure; a loud grinding or rattling suggests a mechanical jam or bearing failure.

Drain pump replacement is generally not a DIY repair, as it requires disassembling significant portions of the machine's cabinet on most models. Cost typically runs $150–$250 including parts and labour. Our team at N Appliance Repair covers washer repair across all of Toronto and the GTA with same-day availability on most calls.

Safety Note: Never attempt to force a spin cycle on a washer full of water. The unbalanced load combined with excess weight can damage the drive system and make the eventual repair more expensive.

When to Call a Professional

Call a technician if:

  • The filter is clean and the hose is clear, but draining still fails
  • You hear grinding, burning, or no sound at all from the pump area
  • The machine displays error codes that persist after basic troubleshooting
  • You see water on the floor or any signs of a water connection leak

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my washer not draining completely?
Partial draining is usually caused by a partially clogged drain pump filter or a kinked drain hose. Full blockage of the filter, a failed lid switch on top-loaders, or a seized pump motor can cause zero drainage.
Can I run a washer that won't drain?
No. Running a washer with a drainage problem risks motor burnout, mould growth inside the drum, and possible flooding. Address the issue before the next wash cycle.
How much does washer drain repair cost in Toronto?
Clearing a clogged pump filter is often no cost beyond a service call ($80). Replacing a drain pump typically costs $150–$220 including parts and labour in Toronto.
How do I manually drain my washer?
For front-loaders, place a shallow pan beneath the small access door at the bottom front, unscrew the drain plug slowly, and let water flow into the pan. For top-loaders, use a wet-dry vacuum inserted into the drum after removing any remaining garments.
